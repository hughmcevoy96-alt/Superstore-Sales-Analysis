# Superstore - Business Insights

## Executive Summary

This report analyses the Superstore e-commerce dataset, focusing on trends in sales, profitability of products and sub-categories, and efficiency in order processing, with the aim of providing actionable insights to support strategic decision-making by the company's directors. Three interactive Power BI dashboards are provided (see screenshots) focusing on revenue, profit and shipping performance for the years 2014-2017.

- A yearly surge in revenue being generated by the Superstore is seen (an average increase by a factor of 2.48 from Q1 to Q4), while no appreciable drop in average lead time over this period is observed.

- Large losses on individual sales were identified, most notably on Cubify CubeX 3D Printers, and on items in the sub-category of tables. A reconsideration of pricing strategies for these items is proposed.

- A discounting upper limit of 40% is proposed as a general rule, as the data suggests completely eroded profit margins above this threshold. This data also suggests there could be other factors at play surrounding the need for high discounting levels, such as the necessity for inventory clear-outs.

## Objectives of Analysis

The Superstore has requested a full analysis of its e-commerce data focusing primarily on the following areas:

### Revenue

- General trend in revenue figures over the years 2014-2017.
- Performance of individual product categories and sub-categories.

### Profit

- Most profitable products.
- Profitability of sub-categories of products.
- An analysis of the various discount levels being applied and how this has been interacting with profit.

### Shipping and Customer Segmentation

- Investigation into potential differences in lead times (number of days from receipt of an order to day the order is shipped) across the product categories - Furniture, Office Supplies and Technology - and across the different shipping modes - First Class, Second Class and Standard Class.
- Investigation into potential quarterly variability of lead times.
- Success rate of "Same Day Delivery" shipping mode.
- A breakdown of revenue based on customer segmentation and geographic location.

## Analysis

![Revenue Breakdown - Dashboard](../Dashboards/Revenue%20Breakdown%20-%20Dashboard.png)

Revenue is growing steadily with total sales figures increasing from \$484K in 2014, to \$733K in 2017. The data demonstrates a marked quarterly difference - Q4 generated 2.48 times more revenue than Q1 on average. In 2017 for example, total revenue saw an increase from \$123K in Q1 to \$280K in Q4.

There is no clear product category that dominates the overall sales of the store, with each performing solidly. For each of the 4 years in question, chairs have consistently been accounting for approximately 45% of total revenue of furniture sold, storage and binders have been contributing most heavily to office supplies at approximately 30% each, and phones have been contributing most heavily to revenue generated from technology, at approximately 40%.

![Profit - Dashboard](../Dashboards/Profit%20-%20Dashboard.png)

When it comes to profit generated by individual products in the years 2014-2017, the Canon imageCLASS 2200 Advanced Copier has been a clear front runner, generating over \$25K, which is more than three times the profit generated by the next best performing product. Copiers are the top performing sub-category when it comes to profit, with the Canon imageClass 2200 contributing almost half of the \$56K total.

![Tables Highlight Screenshot](../Dashboards/Tables%20Highlight%20Screenshot.png)

The sub-category of tables has underperformed by making an overall loss of almost \$18K over the four years in question. As can be seen above, an individual sale of the Chromecraft Bull-Nose Wood Oval Conference Tables & Bases made a loss of over \$1,800 while being discounted by 40%.

Despite machines being one of the less profitable sub-categories of items, falling far short of the average profit generated, four machines feature in the top ten most profitable products. On closer inspection, it can be seen by drilling down to the machines sub-category that huge losses have been made on sales of products in this sub-category - in particular, a loss of almost \$6,600 was made on a sale of Cubify CubeX 3D Printers. In this sale, a discount of 70% was applied.

![Machines Highlight Screenshot](../Dashboards/Machines%20Highlight%20Screenshot.png)

In general, it can be seen from the scatterplots that there is a negative correlation between the discount being applied and the profit of a given sale. It is noteworthy that in the instances where a product is discounted at a rate exceeding 40%, the sale has never resulted in a positive profit. This is something to be aware of going forward for future discounting strategies - an upper discounting limit of 40% should perhaps be considered, with this limit being exceeded only in the case of much needed inventory clear-out.

![Shipping and Customer Segmentation](../Dashboards/Shipping%20and%20Customer%20Segmentation%20-%20Dashboard.png)

For each of the years 2014-2017, there is no meaningful difference in lead times across the three product categories, with the average time in each being broadly similar and remaining constant at just under 4 days. This suggests that there are no obvious issues in order processing efficiency in any of these three departments. The average lead time for all products across each of the shipping modes also seems to follow an expected pattern at 5 days for Standard Class, 3.2 days for Second Class and 2.2 days for First Class. The success rate of the Same Day Delivery shipping mode has been high at an average of 96%, although has trended slightly downwards from an average of 98% in 2014, to 93% in 2017.

Furthermore, as can be seen in the line chart, the average lead time for all products across the four-year timespan does not seem to follow any particular periodicity in relation to the quarters, oscillating for the most part between 3.5 and 4.5 days. Thus the expectation of an increase in lead times in the busier latter half of each year seems to be unfounded.

Year on year, consumers contribute approximately half of all e-commerce sales, with corporate customers slightly lower at 30%, and home office customers making up the minority at just under 20%. The geographic spread of these customers is widespread across the entirety of the US and seems to align with the general population density of the country.

## Key Findings

- The Superstore generates revenue from online sales approximately equally across each of the three product categories - Furniture, Office Supplies and Technology. Revenue has trended upwards from \$484K in 2014 to \$733K in 2017 and follows a yearly periodic pattern - from Q1 to Q4, e-commerce sales have risen by an average factor of 2.48 in 2014-2017.

- Copiers, phones and accessories have been the most profitable sub-categories of products sold by the Superstore, with sales of the Canon imageCLASS 2200 Advanced Copier alone generating almost \$56K in profit. Large losses were seen on individual sales of Cubify CubeX 3D Printers, and the furniture sub-category of tables performed the worst, making an overall loss of  \$18K. High levels of discounting seem to be the culprit for these losses, but perhaps other factors are at play, such as other benefits arising from obsolete inventory clear-out.

- The consumer segment of customers contributes to approximately half of all revenue generated by the Superstore, with an even geographic spread of buyers. The Superstore demonstrates no drop in performance in order processing times throughout the duration of the year, and the lead times across shipping modes is consistent with targets. A slight drop in the success rate of "Same Day Delivery" shipping mode has been seen, from 98% in 2014 to 93% in 2017.

## Recommendations

- Continued equal investment across each of the three product categories should be maintained as each is performing to a high standard.

- A discounting upper limit of 40% should be considered to mitigate large losses being incurred, as the data shows only negative returns above this level. However, perhaps a deeper look at data relating to other factors at play here should be considered, such as the benefits to the Superstore of keeping inventory levels low.

- Pricing strategies should be reconsidered for individual items such as Cubify CubeX 3D Printers and also across the board for tables being sold by the Superstore, in order to limit losses being made by these items in future.
